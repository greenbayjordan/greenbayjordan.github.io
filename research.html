Research Projects

The duality of students and faculty: Illustrating the sociological utility of doctoral committee data
with Kevin Lewis

Collaborating with Kevin Lewis, Jordan analyzed the composition of dissertation committees at a large, research university. This dataset allowed for understanding of gender, faculty position, and other imbalances on dissertation committees and their corresponding consequences for students. This data is novel and aims to lead the way for future sociologists of education to analyze the social network of dissertation committees. This paper was accepted to Socius in September 2020.

The experiences of early career scholars
with Mary Blair-Loy

At The Center for Research on Gender in STEM, Jordan studied early career scholars in science, technology, engineering, math, and medical (STEM) fields. Her research team explored the implicit barriers women face in the application process for tenure track faculty positions in STEM. She specifically coded tenure-track faculty job talk video recordings to see the types and tones of interruptions during the job talk. She also managed undergraduate coders, analyzed the data in SPSS and Stata, and produced scholarly texts for publication. This research collaboration provided Jordan with ample quantitative methods skills and knowledge of academic writing processes for her future solo work.

The honors distinction
Through participant observation and interviewing, Jordan analyzed the advantages conferred by students enrolled in three honors programs. This research culminated in March 2018. Jordan received an Outstanding Paper Award for this project. See the presentation here. Below is the abstract:

Abstract
For undergraduates, enrolling in an honors program is an opportunity to produce guided independent research. In addition to research skills, participation in the honors program yields many identity and resource advantages. This paper articulates how the honors program is a form of educational sorting similar to “advanced” secondary school tracks: students who have historically been advantaged continue to gain advantages that support their expected achievements. When those who join the honors program are those with confidence and ability predicated by their prior track, the honors program reproduces inequality. Through roughly 40 hours of observation and 15 interviews, I studied the subtle advantages honors students receive.
